Blockbuster Video

From thousands of locations world wide to a single solitary video store.

One of Blockbuster's stores that closed.

Here's a time line of the rise and fall of Blockbuster Video:

  • 1985: David Cook purchased a video store franchise in Dallas, Texas known as Video Works. However, the franchise did not allow him to decorate the store in a blue and yellow design, so he left and opened the first Blockbuster Video store. The store had thousands of VHS and Beta tapes.

  • 1987: Blockbuster won a court case against Nintendo that paved the way for video game rentals.

  • 1993: Fearing new technologies, Blockbuster invests in Viacom.

  • 1994: Blockbuster is bought by Viacom for $8.4 billion.

  • 1996: By this time, Blockbuster had acquired several competitors.

  • 1998: Blockbuster was offered an exclusive DVD rental deal from Warner Borthers that would allow them to rent out new movies before they were available for purchase.

  • 2000: The year 2000 was a pivital time for the the company. Blockbuster partnered with Enron to create a video-on-demand service that ultimately didn't work out. Also, the company had the opportunity to purchase Netflix for $50 million.

  • 2004: Blockbuster introduces an online DVD subscription on their website. This was the year that Blockbuster peaked, having more 9,000 stores in the U.S.

  • 2007: Blockbuster introduces Total Access as a strategy against Netflix. It was similar in many ways to Netflix. Initially the company lost money but hoped that it would draw in enough subscribers to compensate for the losses. Fearing the losses, the company eventually raised prices which led to little growth for online subscribers.

  • 2010: By this time, several stores had closed, due to the increasing competition from Netflix and Redbox. Blockbuster had reduced the number of stores in the U.S. to 3,425 and eventually filed for bankruptcy.

  • 2011: Dish Network purchased Blockbuster for $320 million and said it would keep 500 stores open. It introduces Blockbuster on Demand video streaming service through Dish.

  • 2012: Dish announced it would close 90% of Blockbuster stores.

  • 2015: Dish ends its Blockbuster on demand video streaming service and closes most stores.

  • 2019: The only Blockbuster store remaining is found in Bend, Oregon.


Blockbuster, if it isn't already, is going to go into the Harvard Business Review for how not to run a business, or how to run a business into the ground.

-- Ken Tisher, former Blockbuster store owner
